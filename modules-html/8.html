Professionalism

Quick Question

What should you wear to a job interview?

  • What employees at the company wear to their more formal meetings

    This is a good gauge! If you’re in doubt, wear a suit, button-down shirt, dress pants, or conservative skirt/dress.

  • Jeans and a t-shirt because that’s what the team photos online show employees wearing

    Even if it’s a casual work environment, you want to err on the safe side and dress up a bit. In this situation, a button-down and nice pants would work.

  • That three-piece suit I wore to my sister’s wedding that is a bit too tight for me now

    A three piece suit it probably too dressed up for most interviews, especially entry level. You also don’t want to be uncomfortable in clothes that don’t fit perfectly — it might distract you during the interview.

  • My hot pink Hawaiian shirt with unicorns shooting laser beams

    More daring colors or prints and interesting accessories might work for a more creative company or position, but in general it might be smart to veer away from this. More muted colors will keep the focus on your conversation instead of what you’re wearing.

Let's sort this all out

Sort the following guidance into what you should do before you head out for your interview, when you walk in the door, during the interview, and after the interview:

Get directions to the interview location
Leave early enough to accommodate unexpected traffic or other delays
If you arrive more than 10-15 minutes early, find a nearby coffee shop or sitting area to spend the extra time. You don't want to be awkwardly waiting for an hour at their office.
If you will arrive late - which you really shouldn't - call in to let them know you'll be late, and be ready to give a good reason for your tardiness. You might still be able to do the interview or reschedule it, despite being late, if you show that you're eager and give a good reason.
If you can't make it at all, you must call in to cancel as soon as you know you can't make it, and try to reschedule. If you simply disappear off the radar, you'll probably never get a second chance, and might even hurt your reputation down the line with other opportunities!
Turn off your cell phone!
Greet everyone you meet with courtesy and professionalism. Work to make a good impression on the receptionist or other employees. There's always a chance they'll share their impressions with your interviewer later on.
Have a firm handshake. A weak handshake conveys lack of confidence.
Remember, everyone is rooting for you! They want you to be the one they hire!
Keep the big picture story in mind as you answer the questions.
Laugh, smile, and maintain eye contact.
Try to find common ground and relate to your interviewer.
Use humor (keep it tasteful though).
Be gracious and grateful.
Be confident. Project your voice, lean forward, use hand gestures, have energy, and have fun!
Match the pace, tone, and style of the interviewer.
Be concise. Try to avoid talking for more than 2 minutes straight.
Say goodbye and thank everyone for their time.
Send a note by e-mail later in the day to thank everyone again for their time and consideration.
Follow up with your interviewer if you don't hear back.

What if it doesn't feel right?

Much of this guidance follows white normative culture as most companies in the this country operate by these norms.

Operating under dominant culture norms that aren't your own can feel strange. Try practicing these strategies, but only as long as you're okay with it:

MAKE EYE CONTACT

Try to look people in the eyes when you're speaking to them. If you're responding to multiple people, try to create eye contact with each person for about one thought or sentence so everyone feels included.

SHAKE HANDS

A firm handshake upon meeting and when you leave is typically expected. Try to practice your handshake with a friend or mentor if you feel less than comfortable.

MIND YOUR POSTURE AND OPEN BODY LANGUAGE

Interviewers read your body language for cues about your disposition. They like to see you sit up straight and keep your shoulders open and relaxed (not hunched or tight). Avoid fidgeting with your hands or with objects such as a pen, your hair, or your clothes. This may be seen as insecure or distracting. Let your hands rest on the table or in your lap when you're not gesticulating.

SPEAK DIRECTLY AND CONFIDENTLY

As you respond to interview questions, think about framing and what you did rather than what the group did. White normative culture places a lot of value on the individual, and interviewers likely want to hear about your specific role in the scenario or challenge you're describing.
