Quick question

Jerrod is writing bullet points in his resume under his experience of serving as the Online Sports Editor for his college newspaper. Which of these bullets is the strongest?

  • Increased the online views of sports news articles by 50%

    Yes! He uses an action verb and shows the quantifiable outcome of his efforts (50%). Sounds impressive!

  • Was the Online Sports Editor for two years

    He doesn’t start with an action verb. Also, he can include the dates of his involvement above where he begins writing bullets of his accomplishments.

  • Reading articles that sports contributors send me

    Instead of “Reading,” he should use the present or past tense “Read.” He shouldn’t use the first person, “me,” and this bullet could be stronger if he included a quantitative achievement such as “Read and edited ten contributors’ articles a week.”

  • Coordinated the publication of the online sports section

    This point could be more specific so we really understand his achievements: Who did he coordinate? Did he manage anyone? How often did he do this?

HOW TO MASTER THE ART OF THE COVER LETTER

Quick Question

Why does your job application need a cover letter in addition to the resume?

  • To tell a story that your resume can’t to convince the hiring manager that you are perfect for the role
  • To inform the hiring manager of who is sending the resume
  • To repeat what you said in your resume
  • To list all the experience that didn’t fit in your resume

Where Cover Letters Make a Difference

The beauty of a cover letter is that you can craft a compelling story of who you are that the restrictive format of a resume just doesn’t allow.

You can highlight personality traits, strengths, soft skills (e.g. eagerness to learn new things, ability to work independently), why you’re insanely likeable, why you’re going to fit in, why you’re going to perform incredibly well, and why your experience and skills are relevant to the role you’re applying for.

KEEP ALL VERSIONS OF YOUR RESUME AND COVER LETTER

Each application you send will be tailored to the opportunity you’re applying for. But that doesn’t mean you need to start each resume and cover letter from scratch.

Set up your system now

  1. Save a master resume as a Word or Google doc that includes all of your experience in it. It might be longer than a page, and that’s fine, because you’ll never send your master resume out in an application.
  2. When applying for an opportunity, cut and paste relevant opportunities from the master resume into the version of your resume for that application. Save all those versions in one place and name the files in a way that helps you keep track.
  3. Likewise, move all your cover letters all to one place and cut and paste relevant sections into any new cover letter you’re working on. Just remember to always proofread carefully so that you don’t end up using the wrong company or person name!
