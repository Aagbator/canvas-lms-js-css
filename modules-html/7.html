Before going to my interview, I made sure that I’ve done a bit of research beforehand on the company or organization I am trying to intern for. I also make sure to jot down a few questions I plan to ask the interviewer beforehand. Some of those questions include: What does a typical day in this organization look like for you? And why do you enjoy working here?

What I stress to dedicate more time to is preparing for the most common questions the interviewers may ask you. And those questions include: What do you know about our organization or company? Why do you want this job? Why should we hire you? And why are you different than the other people we are talking to? By already having answers to the questions they are going to ask you, employers can learn that you are serious, dedicated, and willing to go above and beyond the bare minimum if given the opportunity to be an intern for their company.

Secondly, I make sure to prepare for the interview mentally. That way I can avoid my mind going blank if I’m too nervous. Preparation for me looks like, thinking of examples, experiences, awards and responsibilities I can mention in my interview that I did not have space for or to elaborate on in my resume. All in all, my goal is to avoid regurgitating what is already in my resume, but to instead give life to that piece of paper.

If you were interviewing a candidate to hire for an open role on your team, what would you definitely want to hear from the potential employee?

  • How her skills and prior experiences will ensure she can do this job well

    Yes! You care most of all that she is a good fit for this role and will help fill the needs on your team.

  • About what isn’t going well at her current job

    Complaining never looks good. It might make you think, is she going to have those same struggles at this job

  • About her college GPA and all of the awards she has received

    If she weaved these accomplishments into her overall narrative of why she would be a good fit, that’s one thing. But listing out hr GPA and awards just makes her sound full of herself. And those accomplishments are already listed on her resume.

  • The challenges she faced as a child

    If a challenge she faced as a child reveals some mindset, skill, or experience necessary for this job, then it would be a story to mention in context. Otherwise, going into her life story sounds irrelevant and like a waste of precious interview time.

Interviewers love to ask this question at the beginning of interviews to see if you’re able to focus when responding to such an unstructured question.     Answering questions with stories is more compelling, memorable, and believable. Your responses to interview questions are really just stories, at the end of the day. There are two storytelling frameworks you can use when answering interview questions. The point of using a framework is to keep your stories focused. The last thing you want to do is ramble on and never get to the point. The first framework is called SYRCL (pronounced circle). Use this framework to answer behavioral questions,which aim to learn how you operate, learn, and deal with adversity. Some examples of these questions might be: What is a recent accomplishment you are proud of and why? When have you worked with a team and failed? How would you work with a teammate who isn't producing great work? How do you motivate someone who doesn't have the skills, ability, or motivations that you have? What would people say about your leadership style?
    Quick Question

    Match what each element of SYRCL should answer.

    SITUATION Pick one situation you were in and describe it. Stay focused on getting the most out of that one situation and avoid sharing more than one example unless asked. You may confuse or dilute your message if you tell more than one.
    YOUR ROLE What choices or decisions did you make? What did you do?
    RESULTS What happened as a result of what you did? What were the outcomes?
    CHALLENGES What challenges did you face? 
    WHAT YOU'VE LEARNED< What lessons did you learn along the way? What did you learn about yourself, your strengths, your skills and the value you bring?

    BE READY TO ASK GOOD QUESTIONS

    Quick Question What should an interview feel like? A conversation Pulling teeth An interrogation A quick chat A test DONE
    That’s right - an interview is a two-way street. You might say something that sparks an idea or memory for the interviewer, and you will have time to ask questions too. The best interviewers feel like a seamless conversation. At the end of the interview, your interviewer will undoubtedly ask, “Do you have any questions?” However, they're not really asking...interviewers expect you to have questions, and they are often evaluating the quality of these questions to see if you did your research. You’ll need to come to the interview prepared with some excellent questions.
    Quick Question Sort these questions into good questions to ask the end of the interview and bad questions. What were the biggest challenges you faced during your first year here? What qualities do you value most in your junior team members? What is the most important advice you give to new hires? What is your favorite part of working at X company? When and how should I follow up after this interview? How do you feel about the President's position on... How do you think your company is doing based on the recent big change in its stock price? What's your vacation policy? How much does this position pay? How long after I join should I ask for a raise? What time do people usually leave the office? DONE

    You’ll notice that these good and bad questions fall into the following categories:

    GOOD BAD
    • Simple
    • Genuine
    • Show your interest in the job
    • Builds a connection with the interviewer (professionally)
    • Political
    • Controversial
    • Company politics
    • Lifestyle (e.g. hours people work, salary) - Only ask these if you get an offer!
